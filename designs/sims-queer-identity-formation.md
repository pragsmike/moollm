## Origin Story: The Oakland Fire

> *"Oakland 1991. Winds from Diablo Valley push a brush fire through the Hiller Highlands through the Bay Area. The brush fire is soon to become one of the deadliest and most destructive wildfires in California history."*

Will Wright, creator of SimCity and SimAnt, lost everything in the 1991 Oakland Hills fire.

> *"In the process of rebuilding his own life, Will Wright thought: What if life was a game? What if I could play a person playing the computer on my computer?"*

**And thus "Dollhouse Simulator" was born.**

The idea: create a game that simulates the choices and consequences of life.

> *"You can poop. You could pee. Anything was possible."*

### The Name Problem

Throughout the 90s, Will Wright workshopped the project. But Maxis leadership kept pushing back:

> *"A dollhouse simulator? Can you make it more masculine?"*

**And thus "Home Tactics: The Experimental Domestic Simulator" was born.**

> *"Yeah, finally a game for men! Wait a second — wasn't Joseph Stalin a man? We can't do that."*

**And thus The Sims was born.**

## The Accidental Gays

By 1998, The Sims development team "still didn't know what to do with gay people."

> *"What are gay people anyways? Is that a band or something?"*

The team faced a **gargantuan task**: simulating social reality — which is embedded in political and social systems. How do you simulate queerness? What does it even mean?

### Patrick J Barrett Joins

In 1998, certified gay Patrick J Barrett joined the team. Head programmer Jamie Doornbos went on vacation, leaving Jim Mackraz in charge.

> *"To pass the time while the boss was away, Mackraz handed Barrett some nice light work just to keep him occupied while the boss was gone. You know, just the entire outline of how social interactions work in the game."*

> *"Barrett, being a gay man himself, figured that gay people existing probably made sense. So he coded gay people back into the game."*

### The Team's Reaction

> *"What did the team think? They just pretty much ignored it. After a while, everyone was just used to the design being there. It was widely expected that EA would just kill it anyways."*

### ⚠️ Historical Correction: The Full Story

**The video essay's account is simplified and partially incorrect.** The actual history is more nuanced:

#### The Original Prototype Problem

The original code prototype had a problematic implementation: **lesbian kisses resulted in slapping** — the game treated same-sex romantic advances as rejections by default.

#### Don Hopkins Throws Down the Gauntlet

During the design document review process, **Don Hopkins** — also gay, as it happens — **called this out** and challenged the team to fix it. This wasn't a "risk-averse decision to exclude" — it was an oversight in the prototype that Don identified and pushed back against.

#### The Team Accepts

The design team was **open to Don's challenge and accepted it**. Same-sex relationships were incorporated into the design vision.

#### Representation → Inclusivity → Success

This is the pattern: having **women and LGBTQ+ people on the team** meant someone was in the room to notice when the simulation didn't reflect reality. Don saw the slapping behavior and said "no." Patrick saw the social interaction code and thought "gay people exist."

The result? The Sims became one of the best-selling franchises in gaming history, generating **over $5 billion in revenue** for EA. Inclusivity wasn't a risk — it was the foundation of the game's success. The E3 lesbian kiss didn't kill the project; it saved it.

#### Patrick's Parallel Implementation

Patrick J Barrett, working independently and likely unaware of the design doc discussions, implemented same-sex relationships in his own way when given the social interaction code.

**What actually happened:**

| Stream | Source | Outcome |
|--------|--------|---------|
| **Original prototype** | Early code | Lesbian kisses → slapping (broken) |
| **Design review** | Don Hopkins' feedback | Called out the problem, team accepted fix |
| **Patrick's coding** | Independent implementation | Coded same-sex romance his own way |
| **Convergence** | E3 demo | Lesbian kiss validated the inclusive vision |

**Key insight:** The inclusion of queer relationships was **overdetermined** — Don Hopkins pushed for it in design review, the team accepted it, AND Patrick independently implemented it. Multiple paths led to the same destination because it was simply the right design choice for a life simulation.

#### Don Hopkins on the Queer Theory Analysis

> *"The queer theory stuff in the essay is great. I did not have the vocabulary and background to express that interpretation verbally, but I agree with it and it is what I was thinking."*
> — Don Hopkins, 2026

This validates Avila's theoretical framework: the designers who actually built The Sims were operating from the same intuitions that Butler, Althusser, and Lacan articulated academically.

### The Nature vs. Nurture Debate Is a Red Herring

Here's what the academic framing misses: **it doesn't matter**.

The debate about whether sexuality is "innate" or "performed," "born this way" or "socially constructed" — it's a red herring. **Humans have the right to choose who they love regardless of whether it's predetermined or performative or whatever.** You can choose your favorite food or color too. It's a human right. Stop arguing about determinism.

#### Why The Sims' Model Was Good Design

The Sims didn't implement performative sexuality because of Judith Butler. It did it because **it made for better gameplay and self-exploration**.

Not representing sexuality as a fixed or changeable character attribute — just person-to-person relationships and behaviors that can change at any time — meant players could:
- Experiment freely
- Discover things about themselves
- Not be locked into choices made at character creation
- Let relationships emerge organically

This is the same design philosophy Will Wright articulated:

> *"I just kind of optimized for gameplay."*
> — Will Wright

The Sims wasn't trying to make a philosophical statement about essentialism vs. constructivism. It was trying to be a good game. The inclusive design was good design, period — not because it was "realistic" or aligned with any particular theory of sexuality.

#### Don's Design Doc Remarks: Throwing Down the Gauntlet

When Don Hopkins reviewed the prototype and tried to have two women kiss, the would-be-kissee **slapped the kisser**. He wrote his response in the design document review (Draft 3, August 7, 1998):

> *"The whole relationship design and implementation (I've looked at the tree code) is Heterosexist and Monosexist. We are going to be expected to do better than that after the SimCopter fiasco and the lip service that Maxis publicly gave in response about not being anti-gay. The code tests to see if the sex of the people trying to romantically interact is the same, and if so, the result is a somewhat violent negative interaction, clearly homophobic. We are definitely going to get flack for that."*

Don proposed a solution:

> *"It would be much more realistic to model it by two numbers from 0 to 100 for each person, which was the likelihood of that person being interested in a romantic interaction with each sex. So you can simply model monosexual heterosexual (which is all we have now), monosexual homosexual (like the guys in SimCopter), bisexual, nonsexual (mother theresa, presumably), and all shades in between (most of the rest of the world's population)."*

And then the gauntlet:

> *"It would make for a much more interesting and realistic game, partially influenced by random factors, and anyone offended by that needs to grow up and get a life, and hopefully our game will help them in that quest. Anyone who is afraid that it might offend the sensibilities of other people (but of course not themselves) is clearly homophobic by proxy but doesn't realize it since they're projecting their homophobia onto other people."*

**"Homophobic by proxy"** — the phrase for people who claim they personally aren't offended, but worry that *others* might be. Don called it out in 1998.

**Don's underlying goal was suicide prevention.** LGBTQ+ youth have historically faced dramatically higher rates of suicide — studies consistently show 3-4x the risk compared to straight peers. Seeing representation, seeing that queer lives are *possible* and *valued*, can literally save lives. When a closeted teenager in a hostile environment plays The Sims and builds a life where two women can marry and raise children and be *happy* — that's not just entertainment. That's a lifeline. That's proof that another way of living exists.

"Hopefully our game will help them in that quest" wasn't just a rhetorical flourish. Don meant it.

#### The Design Response

By Draft 5 (August 31, 1998), the design document reflected Don's feedback:

> *"Same Sex and Opposite Sex relationships: Currently the game only allows heterosexual romance. This will not be the only type available – it just reflects the early stages of implementation. Will is reviewing the code and will make recommendations for how to implement homosexual romance as well."*

#### The Actual Sequence of Events

From Don Hopkins, after discussing with Patrick J. Barrett III:

> *"The initial prototype implementation did not support same sex relationships, and I noticed that, when I tried to have two women kiss, the would-be-kissee slapped the kisser. So I wrote up my opinion that it should support same sex relationships, instead of resulting in homophobic violence, and proposed a straw man 2-dimensional way of modeling it. Subsequent design documents said heterosexual romance would not be the only kind available, and that Will was reviewing the code and would make recommendations on how to implement it."*

> *"Patrick was hired soon after that, and was set to task implementing some social interactions. But Will didn't get back to Patrick and the production database didn't reflect his opinion by the time Patrick started working on it. But Patrick implemented support for same sex relationships anyway, but not by explicitly modeling sexual preference as a property of The Sims' personality — just as a behavior that was possible at any time for any character."*

**The key insight:** Patrick's implementation — sexuality as behavior, not property — turned out to be the design that enabled all those underground lesbian houses and queer awakenings documented in the comments. Not because it was theoretically correct, but because it was **good gameplay**.

#### Why Patrick's Model Was Actually Better

Don's proposed 2D model (0-100 attraction to male, 0-100 attraction to female) was progressive for 1998, but it was still **binary in its conception of gender**. It couldn't account for:

- Non-binary Sims
- User-defined genders
- Trans Sims
- Gender as a spectrum rather than two poles

Patrick's performative model — where any Sim can romantically interact with any other Sim, and preferences emerge from behavior over time — made **no assumptions about gender categories at all**. It was accidentally more inclusive than the "more realistic" 2D model would have been.

This is a case where the simpler, more gameplay-focused implementation was also the more ethically flexible one. The 2D model would have required defining "what counts as male" and "what counts as female" — questions that become increasingly fraught as our understanding of gender evolves.

Patrick's model just asked: "Do these two Sims want to kiss?" That's it. That's the whole question. And it turned out to be enough.

When The Sims 4 finally added explicit sexual orientation settings in 2022, they had to grapple with exactly these limitations — the settings are binary ("attracted to masculine frame / attracted to feminine frame") and many players found them reductive compared to the earlier games' emergent approach.

#### Get a Life

The Sims' tagline was "Get a Life." The irony: the game that let millions of players explore alternative lives, queer lives, lives they couldn't live in reality — that game almost shipped with code that would have slapped those lives away.

Good design saved it. Representation saved it. And the players who spent thousands of hours building underground lesbian houses to hide from their parents? They got their lives.

## The Simprov Wedding Playset: Community-Driven Inclusion

While The Sims 1 allowed same-sex romance, it did **not** allow same-sex marriage — a limitation that frustrated players who wanted full family recognition.

### Indie Fans Step Up

**Don Hopkins**, along with Heather "SimFreaks," Steve "SimSlice," and Donna "SimBabes" — prominent members of the Sims modding community — created the **Simprov Wedding Playset**: custom content that supported gay marriage and non-traditional wedding ceremonies in The Sims 1.

> *"Good news everyone! I asked Heather permission, and she says it's OK for me to give away the huge collection of custom Sims objects I have that includes an archive snapshot of many classic SimFreaks objects, as well as all the unreleased SimProv Wedding Playset objects that Heather and Donna and Steve and I created years ago but never finished and released."*
> — Don Hopkins, 2023

### What It Included

| Object | Function |
|--------|----------|
| **Hope Chest** | Main wedding setup — summons a wedding consultant NPC |
| **Cupid** | Makes your Sim instantly fall in love with anyone — including pets (updated for Pets expansion) |
| **Buddha** | Keeps everyone happy so they don't pee themselves or fall asleep during long ceremonies |
| **Crowd Sitter** | Makes all guests gather and sit down for the ceremony — also useful for "assembling and controlling cults" |
| **Wedding Catalogs** | Order items through the wedding consultant that weren't in normal shopping |
| **Luxury Items** | Wedding beds, buffet with ice dolphin sculpture, gold inlaid glass dining table, elegant dollhouses |

### Not Finished, But Released

The playset was never fully completed — some items remained placeholder programmer art. But years later, Don released the full archive:

- **Download:** https://donhopkins.com/home/DonsSims1Downloads.zip
- **Demo Video:** [Simprov Wedding Play Set](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Mwt5LJlrMe8)
- **Speed Dating Demo:** [Speed Dating With Cupid](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YVUP9OXmHTM)

### The Cupid's Unexpected Power

> *"I recently updated the Cupid to be compatible with the Pets expansion pack, so it now lets you fall in love with any pet in the neighborhood. (You just can't actually marry them — not that there's anything wrong with marrying cats and dogs, but we didn't have the animations for that!)"*
> — Don Hopkins

### SimFreaks and ZombieSims

Heather "SimFreaks" and Steve "SimSlice" — two indie modders who met through the Sims 1 community, fell in love, and got married — later combined their talents to create **ZombieSims**, a mind-blowing fan expansion pack. They've updated it for the 2025 Sims Legacy Collection release.

> *"Heather and Steve were both early Sims 1 fans who each published their own popular web sites with downloadable objects, met through the Sims 1 modding community, then eventually moved in together and got married."*

**Website:** https://zombiesims.com/

## The E3 Lesbian Kiss

Barrett had to create an E3 demo with three scenes. He was in a rush ("as gay people always are") and didn't have time to pre-plan guest actions in the wedding scene.

> *"So we just kind of let the code simulate some interactions."*

**E3 Arrived.**

> *"This was the moment. If The Sims didn't get enough hype, then EA probably would kill the project. Everything was on the line."*

**And then: Lesbians.**

> *"When presenting the wedding scene, two female Sims in the background fell in love and kissed. No one expected these lesbians. But E3 loved it."*

**The Sims instantly went from a project on the verge of being canceled to the talk of the video game community.**

### No Conservative Backlash

Barrett states:

> *"It was also a different time. People weren't so violently for or against same-sex relationships. They didn't go out of the way to find it and react to it. The right-wing press didn't have the platform they have today to scream. There was no Twitter, no Facebook, no blogs. I kind of hoped people would come at night with pitchforks and torches, but it never happened."*

## Programming Queerness

> *"You can't just make a gay button and call it a day."*

### The Problem

Sexual orientation in real life is:
- Complicated
- Historically contingent
- Fluctuating

In a video game, you must **deliberately program queerness in a way that makes sense to a computer.**

> *"Since this program can never fully capture queerness as we experience it in our fruity little reality, the programmer is forced to make firm choices about what it means to experience queerness in the game."*

### The Ideological Position

> *"The programming of queerness will never be a neutral process. It's something that requires an ideological position because you're forced to answer the homoerotic question: What does it mean to be gay?"*

**Questions that must be answered:**

| Question | Complication |
|----------|--------------|
| Does being gay mean you identify as gay? | What about closeted people who identify as straight? |
| Does it mean exclusive same-gender relationships? | What about those without experience or willingly celibate? |
| Is it about attraction? | What about bisexuals who use "gay" as umbrella term? |
| Should attractions be fixed or changeable? | Essentialism vs. constructivism |

## Evolution of Sexual Orientation Mechanics

### The Sims 1 (2000)

**Mechanism:** "Gender Preference" — defined by which genders your Sims flirt with.

- **Not fixed** — changes based on player-led interactions
- **Performed** — player must actively flirt with desired genders
- **No marriage** for same-gender couples (homophobia thing)
- Gay Sims could adopt and raise children, but labeled as "roommates"

**Ideological Implication:**

> *"The programmers implicitly declared that sexual orientation was not something innately programmed into the Sims from birth. Sexual orientation emerges from a Sim's performance of that sexual orientation."*

### The Sims 2 (2004)

- Same-gender relationships allowed
- **Joined Union** instead of marriage
- Gender preference still fluctuates with interaction
- Cheat code revealed Sim sexuality numerically

**Ideological Implication:**

> *"Sexual orientation is not hardwired or innate. It is performed and configured. But some configurations of sexual orientation are ambiguously integrated into the Sims' legal institutions."*

### The Sims 3 (2009)

- **Same-gender marriage allowed!**
- Gender preference still fluctuates
- Full legal recognition

**Ideological Implication — The Disappearance of Queerness:**

> *"If gay Sims now enjoy the same life every other Sim enjoys — do gay Sims actually exist?"*

| What's Missing | Why It Matters |
|----------------|----------------|
| No queer community | No shared history |
| No discrimination | No "other" to be |
| No flags, parades | No identity markers |
| No Macklemore | (Okay maybe that's fine) |

> *"There's no way to recognize queerness as a distinct object, construct, and community. Perhaps gay people in The Sims 3 don't actually exist at all."*

> *"If a gay tree falls in the forest and no one is around to hear it — is it still gay?"*

### The Sims 4 (2014)

**Initial State:** Every Sim was "in practice bisexual." Gender preference either didn't exist or played less role.

> *"If Sims of the same gender flirted with your Sim — and you've been playing your Sim as straight the whole live-laugh-and-loving time — sorry, there is a 100% chance that your straight Sim isn't now."*

**The Forum Wars:**

| Position | Quote |
|----------|-------|
| Pro-spectrum | "I prefer not having gender preferences since sexuality is a spectrum and not an either-or binary option." |
| Anti-game-deciding | "I don't like how this game just decides stuff for your Sims. Katrina keeps getting notices saying she has strong feelings for another girl." |
| Pragmatic | "If the game decides that your Sim is going to pee on the floor, leave your baby out in the snow, and throw tantrums — I'm not sure why romantic feelings for another girl is that big of a deal." |

**The LGBT Mod:**

> *"Why would someone specifically want to add the word 'homosexual' in their game?"*

With mods, players could:
- Have Sims come out as various identities
- Have Sims experience homophobia
- Make Sims homophobic

> *"For some, The Sims 4's utopic vision missed the particularity of the human experience."*

### 2016 Gender Update

Players could customize:
- Physique
- Walk style
- Tone of voice
- Clothing — regardless of gender

> *"It was a good step for trans representation in the game."*

### 2022 Sexual Orientation Update

Finally: explicit sexual orientation settings.

- Attracted to certain genders (binary, unfortunately)
- Whether attraction is "flexible"
- Willingness to "sexually experiment"
- Custom pronouns

**Ideological Implication:**

> *"In this final iteration, queerness is no longer something performed. It is no longer a practice. It is an identity programmed by a set of parameters."*

## Performativity vs. Essentialism

### The Western Default

> *"In the modern Western context, we usually think of queerness as something innate — programmed or biologically wired to make us 'Born This Way.' That's what we've come to accept as the norm."*

### The Original Sims' Departure

> *"The original Sims game departs from this notion. For The Sims, queerness is not something that is essential or innate — but something that is performed. It's a subversive role that disrupts dominant social roles."*

### Judith Butler

> *"The claim that one 'performs' queerness isn't the same as when conservatives claim that homosexuality is a choice."*

Butler argues:
- **All** sexual and gender identities are performative and socially constituted
- Attractions aren't chosen
- But the **act of labeling and categorizing** a person by their sexual attraction is an ideological choice

> *"Perhaps there is no sexuality that predates or transcends our act of labeling it as a sexuality."*

## The Invention of the Homosexual

> *"Humans aren't born knowing the word gay."*

### Historical Fact

> *"The word 'homosexual' only emerged in the 19th century to classify and differentiate people who practice same-gender relations as an 'other.' Not a single person on Earth had ever said the word 'homosexual' in their language until an Austrian guy invented it in the 19th century."*

**The Austrian:** Karl-Maria Kertbeny (1868)

### The Purpose

> *"The concept of the homosexual as an identity rather than a practice was invented in the modern age to classify, differentiate, and ultimately oppress people of non-traditional backgrounds."*

### The Consequence

> *"It's really easy to marginalize people once you categorize groups of people into social categories. The word 'homosexual' created an identity that was then used by psychiatric organizations and governments to pathologize and regulate queer bodies."*

### The Complexity

> *"That isn't to say that the word 'homosexual' can't mean something positive now — but it does mean that the journey to the modern meaning of homosexual is important to remember."*

## Ideology and Ideological State Apparatuses

### What Is Ideology?

> *"In a sociological context, ideology simply describes the ideas and beliefs that justify particular social arrangements."*

> *"Ideology wakes you up at 7am. It joins you on your two-hour commute. And it guides the hand that types at your office desk. It is the force that lets society function the way it does."*

### Louis Althusser

The capitalist state conditions us through **two types of apparatuses:**

| Type | Examples | Method |
|------|----------|--------|
| **Repressive State Apparatus (RSA)** | Police, military | Explicit force and violence |
| **Ideological State Apparatus (ISA)** | Schools, media, churches, family, "those fisting tours brochures you see at three-star hotels" | Cultural conditioning |

### The Sims as ISA

Academic literature characterizes The Sims as an Ideological State Apparatus:

> *"The Sims projects an image of suburban American lifestyle. It obliges us to take that lifestyle for granted. And it forces us to envision success in terms of consumption, status, and career advancement."*

> *"What The Sims does is a process of presenting the player with the representation of the relations with the means of production — that is, according to Althusser, ideology."*

### The Double Alienation

> *"The Sims encourages players to forget about alienation by giving them the illusion of free will, equality of rights — as long as their lives are productive to the system."*

### Althusser's Key Insight

> *"Ideology does not mask the truth of reality — it creates reality."*

Humans create language → Language creates meaning → We're stuck with ideology embedded in our symbols → Ideology makes us into subjects (teachers, students, players, gays, poopers)

## Žižek's Toilets

> *"Everything contains an ideological element. Ideology exists where you would never expect it — even... in the traditional German toilet."*

### The Toilet Ideologies

| Toilet Type | Design | Ideology |
|-------------|--------|----------|
| **German** | Hole at front — shit laid out for inspection | "Reflective thoughtfulness" |
| **French** | Hole at back — shit disappears quickly | "Revolutionary hastiness" |
| **American/Anglo-Saxon** | Basin full of water — shit floats, visible but not inspectable | "Utilitarian pragmatism" |

> *"It is clear that none of these versions can be accounted for in purely utilitarian terms. It involves a certain ideological perception of how the subject should relate to the excrement."*

### The Sims as "Toilet Game"

> *"During the development of the first Sims game, the game was often mockingly referred to as 'the toilet game' — nodding to the fact that much of the game involves sitting on toilets, cleaning toilets, buying toilets, fixing toilets."*

> *"But did the developers ever think about which ideology their toilet would reflect?"*

## The Racist Suburbs

### Will Wright's Intent

> *"Will Wright modeled The Sims after the American suburb."*

> *"Will Wright, creator of The Sims, intended the game to be a satire of American culture: 'It was actually meant to be a satire of U.S. culture and I think most people didn't get that.'"*

> *"The game is supposed to take the American suburban dream and use it to stress you the shit out. Yet however much Wright intended his game to be satirical, the actual critique in the game isn't that obvious. Wright states himself that most people miss the critical aspect of the game."*

### Why the Satire Failed

> *"Perhaps the satire didn't get translated because it's too close to reality. People playing the game in their stressful pursuit of the American dream didn't see the satire in chasing misery. Players think: 'Oh, this is just what life is like.'"*

### The History of Suburbs

**1930s:** Housing shortage, plummeting home ownership (Depression)

**1934:** National Housing Act → Federal Housing Administration → FHA-secured loans

**The Problem:**

> *"America basically only gave out housing loans to white people. The government thought that if they let non-white people buy mortgages in the suburbs that the property values in the area would decline — which actually wasn't true, since African Americans tended to pay more for properties than whites due to the low supply afforded to people of color."*

**Result:**

> *"The Federal Housing Administration subsidized the creation of the suburbs and then purposefully made it white — which helps explain why residential patterns are still pretty segregated by race and why the wealth gap between white and Black Americans is so large. Like, insanely large."*

### The Feminist Critique

> *"Lest we forget how American suburban culture perpetuates the idea of heterosexual female domesticity, and how the nuclear family — the central figure of this suburban culture — has always been used in opposition to queer families and non-traditional queer family styles."*

Betty Friedan's *The Feminine Mystique* (1963) criticized "this suburban cis-heterosexist definition of womanhood common in middle-class American life."

### What's Missing in The Sims

> *"Racism, sexism, and homophobia aren't written into the games — but these elements are certainly embedded in suburban American culture. Yet in The Sims there's no explicit racism, sexism, or homophobia. No traces of the history that helped build the suburbs."*

> *"The Sims' separation of the suburbs from its troubled history isn't just ahistorical — it's ideological."*

## Within or Without

### The Central Question

> *"Does queerness come from within or without?"*

| Position | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Within (Essentialist)** | Queerness is innate, biological, "Born This Way" |
| **Without (Constructivist)** | Queerness is externally imposed, culturally shaped |

### The Western Default

> *"Contemporary Western society compels us to believe that queerness comes from within. We are made to believe that our sexual attractions or gender identities are completely internal experiences that we then project onto the world."*

### The Complication

> *"Our culture also conditions the way we think about our queer identities. So we have to admit that queerness has to be in part externally determined. Queerness also comes from without."*

## Lacan's Mirror Stage

### Who Was Lacan?

> *"Jacques Lacan was a 20th-century French psychoanalyst — which basically means that he was like the gay version of Freud."*

### The Real

> *"When we're born — when we look like those horribly wrinkled potatoes — we enter the world experiencing reality in a way we'll never experience again. As babies, we have no concept of self or other. We experience no separation between the external world, other people, and ourselves. It's the closest we'll ever get to the pure materiality of existence — the Real. Everything is one."*

### The Mirror Stage

> *"One day, a few months after our birth, we see ourselves in the mirror. We see our own image and begin to identify ourselves with that image. We come into existence at that point as a mental self."*

### Love and Hate

> *"We grow to hate and love the image at the same time. We hate it because we believe it is better than ourselves. And we love it because we want to be like the image."*

### The Sims as Mirror

> *"The Sims — this simulation of life — is a mirror to our own reality."*

> *"Queer people play the game. We burn some food, piss ourselves, and perhaps at some point we make two dudes fall in love. Maybe we don't identify with the queerness of that moment because our queer selves haven't come into existence yet. We're still in touch with the Real of The Sims. There is no self and there is no other. There is no distinction between player and game and Sim, between queer and not queer."*

### The Crucial Moment

> *"Player does not enter the game until the crucial moment they begin to identify with it."*

> *"The creation of the self — of the queer self — is defined by a separation. You only become a self when this image of yourself becomes an other. Queer identity only emerges when we begin to identify with an external image of ourselves."*

### Queerness as Other

> *"At some point when you play The Sims, you begin to see yourself. Yet this self-image only comes into existence when we imagine queerness as something outside ourselves — when we see queerness as an other, as separate from the innate experience we've held up to that point."*

> *"Queerness then doesn't appear to us first as an internal experience that only emerges from our brains or our DNA or our hormones. Everything is externally conditioned by language and cultural meaning — including our most intimate and personal identities."*

### The Radical Conclusion

> *"We aren't born queer. We become queer."*

## Survey Responses: Two Play Styles

Avila surveyed his audience with the question:

> *"Has playing The Sims games ever played a role in how you have come to understand your sexuality, your experience with gender, or how you view your gender and sexuality?"*

### Play Style 1: Conscious Identification

Players who **consciously identified** with their Sims — playing themselves.

> *"I made a Sim. It's me. My Sim husband resembles Paul McCartney out of pure coincidence."*

**Uses:**
- Affirm existing queer identity
- Explore queer relationships in hostile environments
- Private, low-risk self-expression

**Quote:**

> *"The character creation — especially since the expanded gender/sex traits options update — has been great for exploring my desired expression and genderqueer identity. It's private and low-risk, but I can see myself and my friends in the characters I make."*

**Quote (Trans experience):**

> *"The high degree of customization in regards to identity, sexuality, presentation, etc. in The Sims 4 has given me the opportunity to better imagine myself as a woman and to play out transition long before I had ever considered it."*

### Play Style 2: Unconscious Discovery

Players who **didn't intentionally** create themselves — but queered their Sims anyway.

> *"I made this group of Sims. They're objects I play around with. And they just happen to resemble the Beatles in a polyamorous follicle."*

**What Happened:**

Some players realized their own queerness through queering third-person Sims. Others queered Sims early but didn't identify as queer until later.

**Quote:**

> *"The Sims played a role in how I understand my sexuality without me realizing it fully. I always played with female Sims falling in love with one another while I was still identifying as straight IRL. Guess I was living my true life through my Sims unconsciously."*

**Quote:**

> *"At the time I didn't really notice it, but looking back it's clear that I would test out my attraction to women on my Sims — spending lots of time creating cute attractive girls on Create-a-Sim, making them flirt, kiss, WooHoo, marry each other, etc. — probably because the game was a place where I felt comfortable doing so. At the time I hadn't consciously noticed my queerness yet. I only knew I had felt different from most people around me, and The Sims gave me an opportunity to explore that without even realizing I was doing so."*

### The Puzzle

> *"If queerness comes from within — as modern society tells us it does — then how do we account for these stories? How does one not know if they're queer if queerness definitely and supposedly emerges from the individual?"*

## MOOLLM Parallels

This video essay illuminates several key MOOLLM design principles:

### 1. Identity as Performance vs. Configuration

| Sims Evolution | MOOLLM Approach |
|----------------|-----------------|
| Sims 1-3: Identity emerges from performance | Characters defined by actions, not just labels |
| Sims 4: Identity as fixed parameters | [`persona`](../skills/persona/) allows both |
| LGBT Mod: Player desires particularity | [`card`](../skills/card/) enables identity representation |

**MOOLLM Insight:** Characters can have declared traits AND emergent behaviors. Identity is both configured and performed.

### 2. The Mirror Stage and Character Play

| Lacan Concept | MOOLLM Implementation |
|---------------|----------------------|
| Mirror Stage | Player sees self in character |
| Identification with Other | [`incarnation`](../skills/incarnation/) skill |
| Separation creates identity | Character has life separate from player |

**MOOLLM enables:**
- Playing as yourself (conscious identification)
- Playing as others who reveal something about yourself (unconscious discovery)
- The crucial moment of recognition

### 3. Ideological Awareness

| Sims Critique | MOOLLM Response |
|---------------|-----------------|
| Simulates capitalist suburban America | Rooms are not locked to one ideology |
| Satire missed by players | Explicit ethical framing in [`representation-ethics`](../skills/representation-ethics/) |
| History erased | Context can be included or omitted consciously |
| No discrimination programmed | Discrimination can exist for narrative purposes with ethical framing |

**MOOLLM Insight:** Awareness of ideology in design. Not pretending neutrality.

### 4. Within or Without — Both

| Question | MOOLLM Answer |
|----------|---------------|
| Does identity come from player or game? | Both, through interaction |
| Is the LLM simulating you or are you directing it? | Mutual constitution |
| Who is the author of the character's voice? | Player + Character + LLM |

The [`incarnation`](../skills/incarnation/) skill explicitly addresses this: characters are played, not possessed. The player's intent and the character's nature co-create the experience.

### 5. Particularity vs. Universality

| Tension | MOOLLM Approach |
|---------|-----------------|
| Sims 3: No queer identity because no difference | MOOLLM allows marked identities |
| Sims 4 initial: Forced bisexuality erases specificity | MOOLLM allows declared orientations |
| LGBT Mod: Players want to name their identities | [`card`](../skills/card/) enables specific identity representation |

**Key Insight:** Some players want utopia (no distinction). Others want particularity (named difference). MOOLLM supports both through configurable detail levels.

### 6. The Accidental Feature

| Patrick J Barrett's Accident | MOOLLM Parallel |
|------------------------------|-----------------|
| Old design doc included gays | Constitution includes inclusivity by default |
| Team "just got used to it" | Skills inherit ethical defaults |
| E3 lesbians saved the project | Play reveals unexpected value |

**MOOLLM Insight:** Design for emergence. Let the system surprise you. The best features may be accidents that become essential.

### 7. Two Play Styles as Design Pattern

| Style | Description | MOOLLM Support |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| **Self-insertion** | "This is me" | Character creation, persona |
| **Third-person manipulation** | "These are my objects" | NPC orchestration, party management |

Both valid. Both can lead to self-discovery. MOOLLM should support seamless switching between them.

### 8. The Toilet Question

> *"Which ideology does your toilet reflect?"*

**MOOLLM asks:** Which ideology does your room reflect? Your character? Your world?

The answer: **Make it explicit.** Don't pretend neutrality. Declare your assumptions. Let players choose or subvert them.

## MOOLLM Implications from Comments

### 1. Safe Spaces Matter

The overwhelming theme of **hiding queer gameplay from parents** underscores the importance of:
- Private play spaces
- The ability to explore without judgment
- Session logs that can be controlled/deleted
- Room-based ethical framing that protects exploration

### 2. Optional Systems

Both the "utopia" preference and the ace/aro testimonies validate MOOLLM's approach of making systems **optional**:
- Needs can be enabled/disabled
- Romance is not required
- Sexuality is not mandated

### 3. Retrospective Recognition

The "looking back" pattern suggests that:
- Players may not understand the significance of their choices until later
- Games can serve as **mirrors** (per Lacan) even when players don't consciously recognize themselves
- The [`incarnation`](../skills/incarnation/) skill should support both conscious and unconscious identification

### 4. Building as Valid Play

The "too busy placing couches" cohort reminds us that:
- Not all players engage with narrative/social systems
- Room building, object placement, and environmental design are first-class activities
- MOOLLM should support **multiple engagement modes**

### 5. Community as Mirror

The comments section itself became a **collective mirror** — players recognizing themselves in each other's stories:

> *"Reading these comments really show all gay people were the same growing up."*

This suggests that MOOLLM's session logs and shareable content can serve similar community-building functions.
